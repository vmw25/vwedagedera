This is an ongoing problem-discovery project. It does not present completed clinical findings, a validated device or a finished product.

## The clinical context

The project examines how decisions and information move through atrial fibrillation care, procedural planning and catheter ablation. The purpose is to build a careful map of the workflow before proposing a technical response.

## Why the problem matters

Cardiac electrophysiology brings together complex clinical decisions, specialist procedures and information from several parts of a patient’s care. A useful product hypothesis must therefore begin with a specific, well-evidenced problem rather than a broad ambition to “improve” the pathway.

## Where uncertainty may arise

This work is testing questions rather than asserting findings. Areas for investigation include how information is gathered, where decisions depend on incomplete context, how plans are communicated and which parts of the workflow create avoidable cognitive or administrative load.

## How I am investigating the workflow

- Mapping the stages of the care and procedural pathway.
- Reviewing relevant clinical and technical literature.
- Separating observed evidence from assumptions and open questions.
- Preparing structured questions for clinical conversations.
- Recording constraints that would shape any responsible product response.

No claim is made here about completed clinician interviews. Clinician perspectives will be described only when they have genuinely been collected and can be shared appropriately.

## Questions being explored

- Which decisions are both important and difficult to make with the information currently available?
- Where is the same information repeatedly collected, reformatted or reinterpreted?
- Which uncertainties are clinical, and which arise from the design of the workflow?
- What would a meaningful improvement look like for clinicians and patients?

## Existing approaches

The next stage is to catalogue current clinical processes, software, decision-support approaches and research tools relevant to each problem area. They will be compared on the problem they address, the evidence behind them and the practical constraints on their use.

## Potential opportunities

Any opportunity described at this stage is a hypothesis. Promising directions would need a clearly defined user, a measurable benefit, appropriate evidence and a realistic path into clinical practice.

## Next steps

1. Refine the workflow map and evidence table.
2. Prioritise a small number of problem hypotheses.
3. Test those hypotheses through appropriately structured clinical conversations.
4. Decide whether any problem is specific and important enough to justify prototyping.
