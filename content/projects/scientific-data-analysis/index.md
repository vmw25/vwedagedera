This page describes analytical capabilities at a deliberately broad level. It excludes confidential compound names, company names, unpublished numerical results, patient-level data and proprietary model details.

## Scientific questions first

Analysis begins by defining the scientific question, the structure of the available data and the limits of what the dataset can support. The method should serve the question rather than determine it.

## Relevant capabilities

- R programming for analysis and visualisation.
- Data cleaning and preparation.
- Interpretation of experimental and clinical datasets.
- Pharmacokinetic analysis.
- Scientific modelling.
- Quantitative interpretation.
- Clear communication of complex findings.

## Working with data

A careful workflow includes checking assumptions, preserving a clear record of transformations, choosing visualisations that support interpretation and distinguishing exploratory patterns from defensible conclusions.

## Modelling

Models are useful simplifications, not substitutes for biological or clinical judgement. I use modelling to examine relationships, test assumptions and communicate how a system may behave within the limits of the available evidence.

## Communicating findings

The final task is to explain what the analysis shows, what remains uncertain and which conclusions are not supported. Clear figures and direct language are central to making technical work useful to other people.

## Next steps

I am continuing to develop my analytical practice in R while building broader programming skills in C, version control and reproducible research workflows.
