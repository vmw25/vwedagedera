> Good healthtech begins with a significant clinical problem, not with a technology searching for a use.

This project documents a practical process for moving from a clinical problem to a product hypothesis without presenting exploratory ideas as launched companies.

## Starting with the problem

The first task is to define one problem precisely: who experiences it, when it occurs, what currently happens and why the existing response may be insufficient. Broad themes are useful for orientation, but they are not yet product opportunities.

## Understanding users and workflows

Different participants may experience the same workflow in different ways. The discovery process therefore considers clinical roles, patient experience, information flow, incentives, constraints and the setting in which any solution would have to work.

## Reviewing current solutions

Before proposing something new, the process examines existing clinical practice, commercial products, research tools and informal workarounds. The aim is to understand what is already useful, where limitations remain and whether the problem is already being addressed well.

## Forming a product hypothesis

A product hypothesis links a specific user and problem to a small proposed change and a testable outcome. It remains an assumption until supported by evidence.

## Testing assumptions

Early tests should target the riskiest assumptions first. Depending on the question, this might involve a workflow walkthrough, evidence review, structured feedback on a concept or a deliberately simple prototype.

## Defining a simple prototype

The prototype should be only detailed enough to answer the current question. It is not evidence of clinical effectiveness, regulatory readiness or product-market fit.

## Measuring whether it helps

Useful measures should reflect the original problem. Examples might concern clarity, time, error prevention, decision confidence or workflow fit, but each measure must be chosen with the relevant users and context rather than assumed in advance.

## Next steps

- Continue identifying and comparing clinical problem hypotheses.
- Document evidence, assumptions and unanswered questions separately.
- Select one narrow problem for a responsible discovery test.
- Define what evidence would justify proceeding, changing direction or stopping.
